Abstract

Joint Speech & Full-Body Motion from Live Audio

Human communication seamlessly integrates speech with non-verbal cues such as gestures and body posture to convey meaning and intent. Existing voice-motion systems rely on fragmented pipelines that introduce cascaded errors and sentence-level latency. We present TTM, the first end-to-end multimodal language model for joint speech and full-body motion generation from live audio with sub-second latency. Its Thinker-Talker-Mover architecture simultaneously drives speech synthesis and motion generation from a shared audio encoding, eliminating cross-stage errors. We propose BucketMoE, a plug-and-play token-length-aware bucketing strategy achieving ∼8× speedup in both training and inference, and TopK, a VQ-VAE codebook-based motion augmentation method yielding ∼7% performance improvement. Experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art co-speech motion quality with real-time responsiveness.

Problem I

No Unified Speech-Motion Model

Cascaded pipelines accumulate errors silently. Without a shared representation, no gradient can jointly optimize audio encoding and motion generation.

Problem II

Sentence-Level Latency

Systems buffer entire utterances before any output, incurring 1.5–3 s latency. Natural turn-taking demands sub-second response times.

Problem III

Limited Speech-Motion Data

BEAT (~60h) and HumanML3D (~28h) are small. Random temporal cropping produces semantically incomplete training fragments.

Method

Thinker · Talker · Mover

TTM extends Qwen3-Omni with a three-component architecture that unifies perception, language reasoning, speech synthesis, and motion generation within a single trainable end-to-end system.

Figure 2

Overview of the TTM architecture. (a) Thinker–Talker–Mover: The Thinker ingests text, VAE-encoded motion, and live audio. Its final-layer representation drives the frozen Talker for streaming speech, while an intermediate representation is routed to the Mover via a trainable Mover Connector for full-body motion generation, with gradients back-propagating into the Thinker. (b) Motion Augmentation: Top-k nearest codebook entries are retrieved per frame; one is sampled to inject a motion synonym without disrupting audio–motion alignment. (c) Training Stages: Stage 1 trains the Mover on text–motion pairs; Stage 2 trains the Mover Connector on audio–motion pairs; Stage 3 jointly fine-tunes the Thinker, Mover, and Mover Connector via LoRA with the Talker frozen.

01

Thinker

Encodes live audio into a hierarchy of hidden representations. Its final state conditions the Talker; an intermediate state (layer 24) routes to the Mover — enabling motion gradients to back-propagate into audio encoding during training.

02

Talker (frozen)

Drives streaming speech synthesis via RVQ with 8 codebooks decoded by Code2Wav. Remains frozen to preserve pretrained speech quality. Its intermediate representations provide cross-modal distillation to the Mover at training time.

03

Mover

Generates full-body motion tokens autoregressively, conditioning on both Thinker and Talker hidden states via a lightweight Mover Connector. Uses corrected HumanML3D SMPL parameters for direct robot retargeting.

Key Innovations
TTM

Cross-Modal Intermediate Coupling

An intermediate Thinker representation is routed simultaneously to both Talker and Mover. During training, motion gradients back-propagate through this shared representation into the audio encoder — a joint optimization signal structurally impossible in any cascaded pipeline.

BucketMoE

∼8× MoE Acceleration — Pure Runtime Patch

Tokens are grouped into K length-aware buckets after expert dispatch and processed as a single batched matrix operation. Reduces iterations from N=128 to K=8 (training) or K=1 (decode). Zero architectural changes, zero extra memory — applicable via a single function call.

TopK

VQ-VAE Codebook Motion Augmentation

Samples uniformly from the k=5 nearest codebook neighbours. These motion synonyms preserve audio-motion temporal alignment by construction while exposing the Mover to a richer training distribution, yielding ∼7% average performance gain.

Figure 3

TopK Motion Augmentation. For each motion frame, the VQ-VAE encoder retrieves the k=5 nearest codebook entries by Euclidean distance. One candidate is sampled uniformly at random rather than always selecting the nearest, introducing a motion synonym (purple) into the token sequence. This codebook-level substitution preserves audio-motion alignment while exposing the model to a richer distribution of motion codes during training.
