Abstract

Joint Speech & Full-Body Motion from Live Audio

Human communication seamlessly integrates speech with non-verbal cues such as gestures and body posture to convey meaning and intent. Existing voice-motion systems rely on fragmented pipelines that introduce cascaded errors and sentence-level latency. We present TTM, the first end-to-end multimodal language model for joint speech and full-body motion generation from live audio with sub-second latency. Its Thinker-Talker-Mover architecture simultaneously drives speech synthesis and motion generation from a shared audio encoding, eliminating cross-stage errors. We propose BucketMoE, achieving ∼8× MoE speedup with zero architectural changes, and TopK, a VQ-VAE codebook augmentation method yielding ∼7% performance gain.

Problem I

No Unified Speech-Motion Model

Cascaded pipelines accumulate errors silently. Without a shared representation, no gradient can jointly optimize audio encoding and motion generation.

Problem II

Sentence-Level Latency

Systems buffer entire utterances before producing any output, incurring 1.5–3 s latency. Natural turn-taking demands sub-second response times.

Problem III

Limited Speech-Motion Data

BEAT (~60h) and HumanML3D (~28h) are small by modern standards. Random temporal cropping produces semantically incomplete training fragments.

Right: TTM unifies everything into a single Thinker-Talker-Mover architecture, streaming synchronized speech and full-body motion at 450 ms latency with no cross-stage errors.

Method

Thinker · Talker · Mover

TTM extends Qwen3-Omni with a three-component architecture that unifies perception, language reasoning, speech synthesis, and motion generation in a single trainable system.

Figure 2: Overview of the TTM architecture. (a) The Thinker's final-layer representation drives the frozen Talker for streaming speech; an intermediate representation routes to the Mover via trainable Mover Connector, with gradients back-propagating into the Thinker. (b) Top-k codebook augmentation. (c) Three-stage training curriculum.

01

Thinker

Encodes live audio into a hierarchy of hidden representations. Its final state conditions the Talker; an intermediate state (layer 24) routes to the Mover — enabling motion gradients to shape audio encoding during training.

02

Talker (frozen)

Drives streaming speech synthesis via RVQ with 8 codebooks, decoded by Code2Wav. Remains frozen to preserve pretrained quality. Its intermediate representations provide cross-modal distillation to the Mover at training time.

03

Mover

Generates full-body motion tokens autoregressively, conditioning on both Thinker and Talker states via a lightweight Mover Connector. Uses corrected HumanML3D SMPL parameters for direct robot retargeting — no post-hoc fitting required.

Key Innovations
TTM

Cross-Modal Intermediate Coupling

An intermediate Thinker representation is routed simultaneously to both Talker and Mover. During training, motion gradients back-propagate through this shared representation into the audio encoder — a joint optimization signal structurally impossible in any cascaded pipeline.

BucketMoE

∼8× MoE Acceleration — Pure Runtime Patch

After standard expert dispatch, tokens are grouped into K length-aware buckets and processed as a single batched matrix operation. Reduces sequential iterations from N=128 to K=8 (training) or K=1 (decode). Zero architectural changes, zero extra memory.

TopK

VQ-VAE Codebook Motion Augmentation

Rather than always selecting the nearest codebook entry, TTM samples uniformly from the k=5 nearest neighbours. These motion synonyms preserve audio-motion temporal alignment by construction, yielding ∼7% average performance gain.

Figure 3: TopK Motion Augmentation. For each motion frame, the VQ-VAE encoder retrieves the k=5 nearest codebook entries by Euclidean distance. One is sampled uniformly at random, introducing a motion synonym (purple) into the token sequence. Temporal alignment is preserved by construction since only the codebook index is substituted.
